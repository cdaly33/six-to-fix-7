Your Website is Your Brand
Building a Brand Platform
Commercial websites began to proliferate in the late 90s, after many
early popular websites appeared around 1995 including The New York
Times, MSN, Amazon, and Google. Back then the “wow factor" was
palpable, and venture capital was ever-flowing to start-ups that had
anything close to resembling a business model.
Point of View on What a Website Is
With 750 million active websites today, many businesses struggle with
creating a relevant and impactful web presence. Why are so many
websites unfruitful for so many businesses? Or largely ignored by so
many companies?
Websites comprise technology and content; and jockey from being an
"IT thing" to a "Marketing thing." But a website is a “business thing,"
which is likely visited far more than a physical office; and viewed more
than brochures, ads, press releases, and often even social media.
Why are websites often subpar at representing the brand? Why is a
website often regarded as an expense versus a business asset?

Strategy and Objectives
A Website is Not a Tactic
A common business oversight is when owners and executive
management view the website as a tactic; something for which team
members further down the ladder are responsible. Your website is
Marketing (including Advertising, Public Relations, Sales, Sales
Promotion, Events and Experiences), Customer Service, maybe even
Accounts Payable and Receivable along with being your “other” office
that is open 24/7/365 across the globe – the website is all of these
COMBINED. How could a website possibly not be considered strategic?
Thinking About Your Website Strategically
The big questions are always, “What are your business objectives?” and
“What goals can your website help you achieve?”
The thing to really think about regarding your website is this: you own it.
It is your company’s “platform.” You own the domain name, the content,
the strategy around it, and the business value it should be building
including your customer base, brand, and competitive advantage.
Social media is awesome. Google is amazing. Google’s YouTube?
Incredible. Amazon? Gigantic. But, if you build your platform on
somebody else’s platform, they own that platform, and can change the
rules any time they want. Your YouTube Channel? Owned by Google.
Your Customers on Amazon? Those are Amazon’s customers. Facebook
ads bringing likes? Owned by Facebook.
## Page 10
Of course, you should be using all of these channels to grow your
business and build business value. But, driving usage of your website
should be part of the strategic plan to generate leads, deliver content
and demos, foster a great customer experience, and ultimately gain new
customers and help retain and grow existing customers.
The next time you catch yourself or a management member saying,
“Yeah, Donna updates the website when she has time…,” it is time to
think about the website more strategically.

Building an Audience
A Website is Practical
It has never been more difficult to reach an audience. Yet, there are
more tools and platforms with which to communicate than ever before.
An Asset You Own and Control
Google, Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn are amazing mediums, but
owned and controlled by others. You own and control your website.
Likes and followers are great, but a “subscribed” audience wants to
engage with your brand, and provides permission to continue building a
relationship with them. A subscribed audience includes building an
email list, which remains the best converting “channel” in marketing.
“Subscribed” means they want to hear from you.
Algorithms and “rules” change on external platforms; not so on your
website. Unlimited space and content through text, images, video,
audio, and interactivity make a website the ultimate brand platform. Did
you know 64% of website visits begin with a search? Nielsen reports the
average American spends nearly 11 hours per day consuming digital
media, and the number of screens per person is increasing.

Usability
Usability Is Not an Option
Most of us visit websites with good usability on a regular basis; it
becomes painfully apparent when you hit a site that was optimized for
Netscape Navigator back in ’99.
Your Brand Reflects the Customer Experience
Imagine a prospective client is coming to your office for a very
important presentation; an incredible opportunity for your company.
Four people will be visiting your office, and the first thing they
experience is that your office is far away and difficult to find. Upon
arrival, they have to park blocks from your office in a crammed parking
lot that costs $15.00 with little space to open the doors without hitting
other parked cars.
You office exterior displays a disheveled welcome mat, tattered awning
over a paint-chipped door, and plants that appear to be from the
Jurassic age. Once inside the office, it gets better: nobody is at the
reception desk, there is no place to sit, the fluorescent lighting is dingy
and buzzing, and nothing adorns the wall – just good ole’ beige paint.
Sound far fetched? It’s not. At all. Not only are there plenty of offices like
that, there are even more websites that fit a similar description. Your
website IS your brand. Your current and future customers want and
need the best experience possible. Why would we give them less?

Inbound Marketing
Thinking Differently About People
Over the last five years, “Inbound Marketing” has become a preferred
way to market due to the prevalence of "digital" and online activity. So,
what is “Inbound Marketing,” and what does it mean to your business?
Pulling Feels Better Than Pushing
Inbound Marketing is different than traditional “outbound
marketing,” in that the methodology is about “pulling”
visitors and prospects to your brand. Contrasted with
traditional "push" tactics like interruptive broadcast
advertising and direct marketing, Inbound strives to
attract visitors and “convert” them into leads, prospects, and ultimately,
customers.
Inbound Marketing strategically uses content to attract visitors because
it is what people are searching for; how they learn and self-educate; and
how brands can establish themselves as customer-centric thought
leaders and authorities, differentiated from the competition.
The Customer Journey
Forming and nurturing a relationship through the “Customer Journey”
(also called, "Buyer's Journey") – the process a visitor takes in moving
from a lead to a prospect to a customer – is the foundation of Inbound
Marketing. It’s the “unselling” approach to selling, by empathizing with
## Page 18
prospects and providing value-added content along their journey, so
they can make educated decisions when they are ready to purchase.
64% of web visits begin with search, and nearly all pre-purchases
involve online research; thus, it makes sense that a website is the
centerpiece of Inbound strategy. SEO, Paid Search (ads), Social Media,
Blogs, are other vitally important Inbound components, but the ultimate
goal is for your website to provide valuable content to prospects and
customers while collecting valuable information from them. This value
exchange allows you to continue to educate them throughout their
Customer Journey.

Content
Content is Still King
Perhaps, the most overlooked aspect of a website is the content that
goes into the website. It is easy to focus on site aesthetics and
functionality, but as they used to say back in the 90s and into the
dotcom boom,“Content is King,” and that has never been truer than
today.
Content Beats Beauty and Function
Technically speaking, a website should feature great content, be
aesthetically pleasing, offer great functionality while being easy-to-use.
Unfortunately, the content piece is often ignored or companies start out
with good intentions, but fall prey to the difficulty of finding a
sustainable way to continually produce fresh and relevant content.
Then What?
Assuming you can drive the right traffic to your website, then what? If
visitors are interested in what you offer, then what? What will they do
upon arrival? What do you want them to do? Do want to educate or
entertain? Do you want visitors to take specific actions that lead to next
steps? If so, then what? If it’s an e-commerce site, do you anticipate
they will go straight for the “buy now” button? Whey they leave the
website, then what? How will you follow-up?
What about the types of content? Do your web pages mostly comprise
## Page 23
text and images? If so, are they broken-up with sub-heads and
digestible copy so that it is easy to read? What about video? Audio?
Downloadable PDFs?
Content is a huge part of the “buyer’s journey” and the Customer
Experience. Content is also a reflection of your brand, and an
opportunity to build differentiation, competitive advantage, and
business value.

Mobile
If it Doesn't Work on Mobile, It Doesn't Work
Prior to 2016, websites functioning optimally on a mobile device was
largely an afterthought. However, 2016 was the first year where internet
usage via mobile devices surpassed computers, thus making the mobile
experience a priority for most companies.
Mobile is the Standard
Certainly, Millennials – those between 18 and 34 are heavy users of
mobile devices, but Gen X’ers and Baby Boomers are heavy mobile
users as well.
In addition to the importance of mobile internet usage is the fact that
the general population has experienced significant behavior shifts in the
last 5-15 years, because of the way we interact with digital technology.
How many times have you watched a show or movie on your television,
while surfing the web and checking email on your smartphone or tablet?
The “second screen” is an important reality, and multiple screens is
readily becoming the “new black.”
Responsive Design and Customer Experience
Responsive design is the discipline of ensuring that a website functions
and operates optimally across all screens and platforms – regardless of
device type. Content is also a big consideration when delivering on
mobile, because the way people interact with and consume media

computer.

regardless of the platform.

owners and executive managers
